The Central Question

Why pancreatic cancer? Why now?

"Can stable RNA molecules released into the blood by cancer cells be used to detect pancreatic cancer earlier and predict patient outcomes?"

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is one of the most lethal cancers largely because it is almost always diagnosed too late. Current blood-based protein markers such as CA19-9 lack sufficient sensitivity and specificity for early-stage disease.

This project asks whether circular RNAs (circRNAs) — highly stable, covalently closed RNA molecules that cancer cells package into exosomes and release into blood — could serve as more reliable, non-invasive biomarkers. Their resistance to degradation makes them uniquely suited for liquid biopsy applications.

Background & Rationale

Three converging fields
of molecular biology

🧫

Exosomes & Liquid Biopsy

Cancer cells communicate by releasing extracellular vesicles (EVs) — especially exosomes — that carry proteins and RNAs mirroring the tumor's biological state. Because exosomes circulate freely in blood, they form the foundation of liquid biopsy: a non-invasive alternative to surgical tissue sampling that can be repeated over time.

🔁

Circular RNA (circRNA) Biology

circRNAs are covalently closed RNA molecules formed by back-splicing. The absence of free 5′ and 3′ ends makes them highly resistant to degradation — an ideal property for circulating biomarkers. Many circRNAs act as microRNA sponges, placing them at the centre of the ceRNA regulatory framework that shapes cancer gene expression.

🦠

Pancreatic Cancer (PDAC)

PDAC is the most common and lethal form of pancreatic cancer, typically diagnosed at advanced stages due to the lack of early biomarkers. Large-scale transcriptomic datasets such as TCGA-PAAD offer a unique computational opportunity to discover prognostic RNA signatures that can later be validated in blood-based liquid biopsy settings.

🕸️

ceRNA Regulatory Networks

The competing endogenous RNA (ceRNA) framework describes how circRNAs sponge miRNAs, freeing downstream mRNA targets from suppression and reshaping cancer gene expression programs. Mapping these networks provides both mechanistic insight and a rational basis for biomarker prioritisation across the circRNA–miRNA–mRNA regulatory chain.

Specific Aims

Three hypotheses driving
this project forward

Each aim builds on the last — from candidate identification through network construction to clinical prognostic relevance.

Aim 01
Hypothesis

Identify PDAC-associated exosomal circRNAs

circRNAs enriched in exosomes are dysregulated in PDAC and reflect the underlying tumor biology, making them viable non-invasive detection candidates that could be measured from a simple blood draw.

Aim 02
Hypothesis

Construct ceRNA regulatory networks

PDAC-associated circRNAs regulate cancer-relevant genes through miRNA-mediated ceRNA mechanisms, providing both mechanistic insight into tumor biology and a network-level basis for biomarker candidate prioritisation.

Aim 03
Hypothesis

Derive a prognostic circRNA signature

A subset of exosomal circRNAs is significantly associated with patient survival in the TCGA-PAAD cohort and can define high-risk molecular subgroups, supporting clinical decision-making and prognostic stratification.

Expected Outcomes

What this project
will produce

01

Novel Biomarker Signature

A small, interpretable panel of exosome-derived circRNAs linked to PDAC prognosis, with biological rationale for each candidate and a clear path to experimental validation.

02

Mechanistic ceRNA Network

A visualized circRNA–miRNA–mRNA interaction network in Cytoscape identifying hub regulators in pancreatic cancer biology and key downstream effector pathways.

03

Reusable Analysis Pipeline

A reproducible, fully documented R-based analysis workflow — complete with clean scripts and README — adaptable to other cancer types and RNA biomarker discovery projects.

04

Research Training

Structured mentorship in translational bioinformatics, network biology, survival analysis, and scientific communication for both medical and computational students.
